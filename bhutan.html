BHUTAN

Posted By: Vinay Kumar
Posted on: 14-03-26

Bhutan, officially the *Kingdom of Bhutan* and often called *Druk Yul* (“Land of the Thunder Dragon”), is a small Himalayan nation known for its unique approach to development and its deep cultural traditions.

    Historical background
  • Bhutan was unified in the early 17th century under Shabdrung Ngawang Namgyal.
  • The Wangchuck dynasty came to power in 1907, establishing a hereditary monarchy.
  • MIn 2008, Bhutan transitioned into a constitutional monarchy, with King Jigme Khesar Namgyel Wangchuck as head of state and a parliamentary democracy in place.
  • Historically isolated, Bhutan carefully opened to the world in the late 20th century, balancing modernization with cultural preservation.
    Culture and society
  • Dzongkha is the official language, though many regional languages are spoken.
  • Buddhism is the dominant religion, shaping Bhutan’s art, architecture, and daily life, while Hinduism is also practiced.
  • Bhutan is famous for its festivals (*tsechus*), traditional dress (*gho* and *kira*), and distinctive fortress‑monasteries (*dzongs*).
  • The country emphasizes *Gross National Happiness* (GNH) as a guiding philosophy, prioritizing well‑being and environmental sustainability over purely economic growth.
    Current situation (2026)
  • Bhutan continues to balance modernization with cultural preservation, carefully managing tourism to protect its environment and traditions.
  • Politically, it remains a stable constitutional monarchy with democratic institutions.
  • Economically, Bhutan relies on hydropower exports, agriculture, and tourism, while facing challenges from climate change and limited industrialization.
  • Socially, Bhutan invests in education and healthcare, guided by its GNH philosophy.

In summary
Bhutan today is a nation that stands out for its deliberate pace of modernization, strong cultural identity, and commitment to happiness and sustainability. Nestled between India and China, it remains one of the world’s most distinctive examples of how tradition and modern governance can coexist.
