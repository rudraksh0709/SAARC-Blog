MALDIVES

Posted By: Vinay Kumar
Posted on: 14-03-26

The Maldives is an island nation in the north‑central Indian Ocean, stretching across more than 500 miles of atolls and coral islands. It is famous for its turquoise waters, coral reefs, and luxury tourism, but also faces pressing environmental and political challenges.

    Historical background
  • The Maldives has been inhabited for centuries, influenced by South Asian, Arab, and African cultures through trade routes.
  • It became a British protectorate in 1887 and gained independence in 1965.
  • In 1968, the monarchy was abolished, and the Maldives became a republic.
  • The current constitution, adopted in 2008, established a presidential system of government.
    Culture and society
  • Dhivehi is the national language, while English is widely used in education and business.
  • Sunni Islam is the official religion, shaping social and cultural life.
  • Traditional music, dance (like bodu beru), and crafts remain important, alongside modern tourism‑driven culture.
  • Malé, the capital, is the political and economic hub.
    Current situation (2026)
  • President Mohamed Muizzu’s 2026 address emphasized transparency, economic stability, and housing as top priorities.
  • Infrastructure projects, including ports and IT hubs, are being advanced to diversify beyond tourism.
  • Foreign policy highlights sovereignty and maritime security, reflecting the Maldives’ strategic location in the Indian Ocean.
  • Climate change remains the greatest existential threat, as rising sea levels endanger the low‑lying islands.

In summary
The Maldives today is a nation balancing its rich cultural heritage and reliance on tourism with urgent environmental and political challenges. Its future depends on how it manages climate risks, economic diversification, and governance reforms.
